3. ConclusionÂ¶

Globally, we have already lost a significant amount of biodiversity and we risk losing much more in the years ahead: even common plant species are becoming less widespread, and rare species are dissapearing entirely. Preservation must occur on the local level and thus actions should be guided by local data. Fortunately the survey results demonstrate that Biel hosts a number of interesting plant communities that provide a foundation for urban ecosystems thanks to the management of the city of Biel/Bienne. The vast majority of species in the public spaces are native plants, which have a higher value to urban ecosystems, and in some areas, floral resources even appear from February to October ensuring resources are available to the first insects that emerge after the winter. At the same time, it is clear that there are significant improvements that can be made to the nature value produced by urban ecosystems.

3.1. The surveys continueÂ¶

Needless to say, the surveys continue in 2022 with some minor changes to the methodology. First, the surveys will only be done with InfoFloraâ€™s excellent mobile applications that make recording plant inventories and determining species convenient and quick. Some data on species density and flowering status will also be recorded and a larger suite of species will be included in the surveys. Some changes to site location and selection will be made with an even stronger focus on covering the diversity of metropolitan Biel, especially areas which were relatively neglected in the first round of surveys.

In order to fully take advantage of all the resources available to make this data valuable, some third party data should be included and the actors responsible shold be consulted for their feedback on the report and methodology. This includes primarily InfoFlora, which houses all plant survey data across Switzerland, and the City of Biel/Bienne which manages and publishes excellent local GIS data with a registry of city trees.

3.3. A few words on the local market for wildflowersÂ¶

If regional biodiversity is to be preserved effectively, then the market for local, source-verified wildflowers must expand. In terms of demand, there is an increasing interest among private individuals in using local wildflowers to create natural gardens that provide habitat and food resources to the larger ecosystem. Public authorities are increasingly engaged in renaturalization projects that should use local, source-verified wildflowers. Indeed, there are an increasing number of companies offering to cut flowering meadows, dry the seed and offer larger scale wildtype wildflower mixes of variable genetic quality. The bias is towards grassland and wildflower meadow species for property developments and agricultural purposes, which leads to the fact that a consumer in Biel/Bienne would be hard pressed to find native wildflower seeds sourced locally.
